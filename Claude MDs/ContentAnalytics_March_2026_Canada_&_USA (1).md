### 1.1 Monthly Box Office Overview

The North American (USA and Canada) theatrical landscape in March 2026 is exhibiting a highly polarized, "feast or famine" environment. Overall, the total domestic gross for the month is trending significantly lower than the same periods in 2024 and 2025, signaling a sluggish start to the spring box office season. However, the back half of the month is experiencing a massive injection of capital. The weekend of March 20–22 is projected to generate over $128 million across the top 10 films, representing a staggering 91% increase over the same weekend in 2025 (which was led by *Snow White*'s $42.2M debut) and sitting just 8% below the pre-pandemic benchmark of 2019.

This month's market context is defined by the post-Oscars transition, where studios pivot from prestige adult dramas to early four-quadrant summer-style blockbusters. The month has seen 109 different films play in theaters—ranging from micro-budget qualifiers to $200M tentpoles—but the wealth is heavily concentrated. The top five spots on the monthly chart are entirely monopolized by the "Big Five" legacy studios (Disney, Paramount, Sony, Universal, Warner Bros.), demonstrating that in the 2026 theatrical ecosystem, massive marketing muscle and Premium Large Format (PLF) exclusivity are the only reliable drivers of foot traffic.

### 1.4 Surprise Hits and Underperformers

**🟢 OVERPERFORMERS**
*   **Project Hail Mary (Amazon MGM)**: Directed by Phil Lord and Chris Miller and starring Ryan Gosling, this $200M sci-fi adaptation of Andy Weir's novel is the undisputed story of the month. It generated a massive $12 million in previews, making it the highest-grossing preview total of 2026 so far, and the second-best ever for a non-sequel/non-franchise film (trailing only 2017's *It* at $13.5M). With a 95% Rotten Tomatoes score and a 98% Popcornmeter rating, word-of-mouth is stellar. It is tracking for a $60M–$65M opening, easily surpassing *Creed III* ($58.7M) to become Amazon MGM's biggest opening weekend ever.
*   **Hoppers (Disney/Pixar)**: Breaking from Pixar's traditional summer release corridor, this original IP directed by Daniel Chong has been a massive success. After the studio struggled to launch new IP theatrically post-pandemic (with last summer's *Elio* opening to a muted $20.8M), *Hoppers* dominated the first half of March, raking in nearly $96 million in its first two weeks. 
*   **Undertone (A24)**: This micro-budget indie horror film opened to $9.3M, vastly outperforming its tracking. It serves as a textbook example of how targeted, low-spend genre films can achieve immediate profitability while massive studio swings fail.

**🔴 UNDERPERFORMERS**
*   **The Bride! (Warner Bros.)**: Maggie Gyllenhaal's $80M–$90M feminist, punk-rock reimagining of the Frankenstein mythos is the biggest bomb of the quarter. Starring Christian Bale and Jessie Buckley, the film suffered a disastrous identity crisis in its marketing—audiences couldn't tell if it was a monster movie, an empowerment thriller, or a Bonnie-and-Clyde love story. It opened to a dismal $13.6M worldwide, earned a toxic "C+" CinemaScore, and plummeted 70% in its second weekend. Industry sources estimate total theatrical losses could approach $90 million.
*   **Crime 101 (Amazon MGM)**: Despite a star-studded cast including Chris Hemsworth, Mark Ruffalo, and Halle Berry, this heist thriller burned through a $93 million budget to open at just $16 million domestically. It marks Hemsworth's worst domestic opening in 8 years and continues Amazon MGM's struggle to turn adult-skewing theatrical releases into profitable ventures.

**🟡 LEGS AND HOLDOVERS**
*   **Scream 7 (Paramount)**: While the film has crossed the $100M+ mark domestically, its legs have been shockingly weak. The film suffered a staggering 74% domestic drop in its second weekend. This indicates severe franchise fatigue; the core fanbase rushed out on opening weekend, but casual moviegoers have entirely rejected the title.

### 1.5 International Box Office

*   **Wuthering Heights (Warner Bros.)**: Emerald Fennell's provocative adaptation starring Margot Robbie and Jacob Elordi is a fascinating case study in international economics. The film has grossed a seemingly solid $226 million worldwide after 5 weeks. However, Warner Bros. spent $80 million on production and a staggering $100 million on a massive global promotional tour. Because of these inflated costs, the film needs over $330 million just to break even, leaving it more than $100 million short and losing momentum rapidly.
*   **Project Hail Mary**: The sci-fi epic is eyeing a $100M+ global opening, with international markets projected to contribute roughly $35M–$40M to the debut weekend. The film's reliance on visual spectacle translates well across borders, though it faces a steep climb to hit its estimated $500 million global break-even point.
